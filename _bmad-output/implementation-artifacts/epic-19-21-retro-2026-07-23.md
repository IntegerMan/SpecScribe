## What Went Well

**The spike → build handoff produced measurable, not theoretical, value.** Story 19.1
shipped a coverage map and no production code; Story 19.2 opened with an
"Implementation Plan — 19.1 findings ingested (Task 0)" section that locked node/edge
vocabulary, direction convention, and NFR8 gating before any code was written. 19.1's
*code-review* decisions then paid out again in 19.2's implementation:

- **D1** (route unattributed follow-ups into an "Unattributed" pseudo-epic bucket)
  surfaced **40 deferred items the first dogfood silently dropped** — 170 → 210 drawn
  nodes. Without the spike's review that ships as quiet data loss.
- **D2** (no manufactured transitive edge) and **D4** (never mint a phantom node from an
  unresolved `SourceKey`, per the `a16ca0f` fix) both landed in code unaltered.

**Live-browser verification caught three real defects the 2,158-test suite structurally
cannot see.** This is the epic pair's strongest quality signal:

1. **21.1** — the *deployed* `traceability.html` scrolled far past its footer. Diagnosed
   against the live site by comparing `documentElement.scrollHeight` (2990px) to
   `body.scrollHeight` (959px), then bisected live to `.table-scroll`'s
   `contain: inline-size` leaking a bounded descendant's unclipped height into the root
   scroll region. Fixed with a two-class selector to win the cascade tie.
2. **21.3** — the squarify layout read `node.value` while file nodes carried churn as
   `.c`; every tile laid out under 1px and the treemap rendered **zero tiles**.
3. **19.2** — the first tab implementation string-split the assembled body at
   `</header>`, swallowing the sidebar closers and collapsing content to a ~300px column.

All three would have passed CI.

**The ADR-creation trigger from Epic 10 fired correctly, once.** Story 19.1's review
deferred an ADR with an explicit condition — *escalate if the convention spreads beyond
Epic 19*. When Epic 24 inherited the carrier→target rule, it became
[ADR 0011](../../docs/adrs/0011-directed-graph-edge-direction-carrier-to-target.md),
recording an in-force convention without changing shipped behavior. That is exactly the
mechanism Epic 10 asked for.

**NFR8 / degrade-honestly discipline held on all five new surfaces.** Every page shares
its nav gate so a link cannot dangle independently; 21.3 built a justified *combined*
`hasEpics && hasDeepAnalytics` gate; 21.1 distinguishes "deferred on purpose" from
"not yet mapped" in its own empty state rather than conflating them.

## Challenges

### 1. The iteration model was mis-framed — and the correction is the finding

Every story in both epics had at least one post-implementation revision round
(19.2: three; 21.1: two, one a post-deploy bug; 21.2: one of five items; 21.3: two, one a
full redesign). The retro opened by framing this as "not one shipped clean."

**The Project Lead corrected the record:** the rounds are the designed workflow, not
leakage past a gate —

> create-story → dev-story → **owner verifies rendered behavior and gives extensive
> commentary** on what is wrong, non-standard across surfaces, or behaving badly →
> iterate the story → **code review passes run only once the whole epic is complete and
> the owner is satisfied.**

Two real consequences follow, and they reframe two other observations:

- **Sibling-commit bundling is structural, not sloppy.** Because reviews run at epic end,
  every review in this pair had to hand-declare "sibling stories excluded from the same
  commit range" (19.2/21.1/21.2/21.3 all landed in overlapping commits, e.g. `b9582a4`).
  Scoping by story File List is the *correct adaptation*, not a workaround. 21.2's review
  dismissing it as a "review-scope hygiene note" was right.
- **The lever is elicitation depth, not round count.** The Project Lead named both causes
  directly: *"I need to see it rendered"* (inherent — no amount of paper design replaces
  the real page with real data) **and** *"elicitation is too shallow."* Stories 21.2 and
  21.3 both carried "Owner-selected design directions (locked at create-story — do not
  re-litigate)" sections, and 21.3's was superseded by a full redesign anyway. Deeper
  elicitation does not remove the round; it changes what the round is *about* — "this
  color is wrong, this unit is missing" instead of "rebuild this as a treemap."

This is the visual-intent elicitation action item, open since Epic 3 and now recurring
across Epics 3, 6, 7, 19, and 21. The reframe is that it was never a rework-prevention
item; it is a **round-depth** item.

## Key Takeaways

1. **The revision rounds are the process, and elicitation depth sets their size.** Deeper
   create-story elicitation (colors, units, density, empty states, controls — not just a
   named silhouette) converts redesign rounds into polish rounds. It will never convert
   them to zero, and should not try to.
2. **Read the ADRs before claiming you are breaking a rule.** Project memory goes stale
   within days; a ratified ADR is the authority. Epic 10 built a creation trigger and
   left the consultation half unbuilt.
3. **Spikes with reviewed decisions pay out in the build.** 19.1's D1 alone recovered 40
   silently-dropped nodes. The value came from the spike being *reviewed*, not merely
   written.
4. **Live-browser verification is not optional polish for visual work.** Three defects in
   five stories were invisible to a 2,158-test suite and visible immediately in a browser.
5. **Concurrency on shared `main` is a permanent working condition.** Every convention —
   verify-after-edit, review-by-File-List, golden-stability-across-runs — is an adaptation
   to it, not a temporary workaround.
